224 \page ODDbase ODD-Description of the basic model
225 
226 The model description follows the ODD protocol (overview,
227 design concepts, details) for describing individualbased
228 models (Grimm et al. 2006).
229 
230 \section Purpose
231 The model is designed to evaluate the response of plant
232 functional type (PFT) diversity towards grazing under
233 different local environmental conditions and differentiated
234 assumptions about plant-plant competition.
235 
236 \section variables State variables and scales
237 The model includes the entities seeds, individual plants
238 and grid cells (Table 1). Seeds are described by the state
239 variables position, age, and mass. Plant individuals are
240 characterized by their position, the mass of three plant
241 compartments (shoot, root and reproductive mass), the
242 duration of resource stress exposure, and they are classified
243 as a certain PFT with specific trait attribute parameters
244 (Table 3). In a simplified model version only two plant
245 compartments, vegetative and reproductive mass, are taken
246 into consideration. Spatially, plants are described by their
247 ‘zone-of-influence’ (ZOI), i.e. a circular area around
248 their location (Schwinning and Weiner 1998, Weiner et
249 al. 2001). Within this area the individual can acquire
250 resources and if the ZOIs of neighbouring plants overlap,
251 the individuals will only compete for resources in the
252 overlapping area. For the three compartment model
253 version we consider two independent ZOIs for a plant’s
254 shoot and root, representing above- and below-ground
255 resource uptake and competition. The ZOIs radii are
256 determined from the biomass of the corresponding plant
257 compartment.
258 
259 In order to simplify spatial calculations of resource
260 competition, ZOIs are projected onto a grid of discrete
261 cells. Grid cells represent 1 cm2. The state of a grid cell is
262 defined by two resource availabilities, above and below
263 ground (in the simplified model version, only one resource
264 is considered). The size of the modelled arena was
265 128 by 128 cm. To avoid edge effects, periodic boundary
266 conditions were used, i.e. the grid essentially was a torus.
267 A model’s time step corresponds to one week; a vegetation
268 period consisted of 30 weeks per year, and simulations
269 were run for 100 years.
270 
271 \section sced Process overview and scheduling
272 The processes resource competition, plant growth and
273 plant mortality are considered within each week of the
274 vegetation period. Seed dispersal and seedling establishment
275 are limited to certain weeks of the year (Table 2).
276 Grazing events occur randomly with a fixed probability
277 which is constant for all weeks. Two processes, winter
278 dieback of above-ground biomass and mortality of seeds
279 are only considered once a year, at the end of the
280 vegetation period (Fig. A1 in Supplementary material
281 Appendix 1). Plant's state variables are synchronously
282 updated within the subroutines for growth, mortality,
283 grazing and winter dieback, i.e. changes to state variables
284 are updated only after all model entities have been
285 processed (Grimm and Railsback 2005).

287 \section emergence Design concepts: emergence
288 All features observed at the community level, such as
289 community composition and diversity, emerged from
290 individual plant-plant interactions, grazing effects at the
291 individual scale, and resource availabilities.
292 
293 \section adapt Design concepts: adaptation
294 In the submodel representing plant growth, aboveand
295 below-ground competition, plants adaptively allocate
296 resources to shoot and root growth in order to balance
297 the uptake of above- and below-ground resources (see
298 \ref growth "Submodel: plant growth and mortality").
299 
300 \section interact Design concepts: interactions
301 Competitive interactions between plant individuals were
302 described using the ZOI approach.
303 
304 \section stoch Design concepts: stochasticity
305 Seed dispersal and establishment, as well as mortality of
306 seeds and plants are modelled stochastically to include
307 demographic noise. Grazing events occur randomly during
308 the vegetation period and the affected plants are chosen
309 randomly, but the individual’s probability of being grazed
310 depends on plant traits (see Submodel: grazing).

315 \section init Initialization
316 Initially, ten seedlings of all 81 PFTs (see section Plant traits
317 and PFT parameterisation) with their respective seedling
318 mass were randomly distributed over the grid. Their
319 germination probability was set to 1.0 to assure equal
320 initial population sizes of all PFTs. A spatially and
321 temporally homogenous distribution of resources (both
322 above- and below-ground) was used in all simulation
323 experiments.

325 \section Input
326 The model does not include any external input of driving
327 environmental variables.

513 \section sprod Submodel: seed production, dispersal and establishment
514 All plants disperse their seeds in week 21 each year. Seed
515 number is determined by dividing reproductive mass by the
516 mass of one seed (Schippers et al. 2001, Lehsten and Kleyer
517 2007). For each seed, dispersal distance is drawn from a lognormal
518 and direction from a uniform distribution (Stoyan
519 and Wagner 2001). To avoid edge effects on the small scale
520 investigated, periodic boundary conditions are used.
521 
522 Germination and seedling establishment are limited to
523 four weeks in autumn directly after dispersal and four weeks
524 in spring of the next year for all PFTs. In between, a winter
525 mortality of 50% of seeds is assumed and all seeds which
526 did not germinate in these two seasons are removed.
527 
528 Seedling recruitment is separated in two consecutive
529 processes: (1) seed germination and (2) seedling competition.
530 Germination is only allowed in grid cells that are not
531 covered by any plant or not covered in the above-ground
532 layer, respectively. In uncovered cells, seeds germinate with
533 a fixed probability (pgerm) and are converted to seedlings. In
534 each cell only a single plant is allowed to establish. Seedling
535 competition is modelled as a weighted lottery, using seed
536 mass as a measure of competitive ability between seedlings
537 (Chesson and Warner 1981, Schippers et al. 2001). The
538 seedling that is chosen for establishment is converted into a
539 plant with a shoot (and root) mass equal to seed mass. All
540 the other seedlings that germinated within the cell die and
541 are removed from the grid.
542 
543 \section graz Submodel: grazing
544 Grazing is modelled as partial removal of an individual’s
545 above-ground biomass. The frequency of grazing is specified
546 by a constant weekly probability (pgraz) of a grazing
547 event. Grazing is a process that acts selectively towards
548 trait attributes such as shoot size and tissue properties.
549 Therefore, for each plant the susceptibility to grazing
550 (sgraz) is calculated as a function of shoot size, geometry
551 and PFT-specific palatability (palat).
552 
553 sgrazmshootf1
554 leafpalat (7)
555 
556 The probability for each plant to be grazed within one week
557 is derived by dividing individual susceptibilities by the
558 current maximum individual susceptibility of all plants. All
559 plants are checked for grazing in random order. In case a
560 plant is grazed, 50% of its shoot mass and its complete
561 reproductive mass are removed. The random choice of
562 plants is repeated without replacement, until 50% of the
563 total (above-ground) biomass on the whole grid has been
564 removed. When all plants have been checked for grazing
565 once, but less than 50% of the total above-ground biomass
566 has been removed, grazing probabilities for all individuals
567 are calculated once more based on Eq. 7 and the whole
568 procedure is repeated until 50% of the total above-ground
569 biomass has been removed or until a residual biomass is
570 reached which is considered ungrazable. This fraction is set
571 to 15 g m2 following (Schwinning and Parsons 1999).
572 This allows a plant individual to be grazed never or several
573 times during one week with a grazing event.
574 
575 In addition to stochastic grazing, each year at the end of
576 the vegetation period 50% of the above-ground mass of all
577 plant individuals is removed to mimic vegetation dieback in
578 winter.

580 \section traits Plant traits and PFT parameterisation
581 The PFTs that are used in the model differ in their
582 attributes for nine plant functional traits (Table 2). These
583 are grouped into four trait syndromes based on well
584 documented tradeoffs and trait correlations. The traits we
585 chose comprise a subset of the 'common core list of plant
586 traits', proposed by Weiher et al. (1999).
587 
588 The growth form of a plant is characterized by the ratio
589 between leaf mass and total shoot mass (fleaf). Plants with a
590 low fleaf use more biomass to build up support tissue (e.g.
591 stems) instead of leave mass. Therefore this trait implicitly
592 includes a tradeoff between plant height and leaf area or
593 between competitive ability for light versus relative growth
594 rate without competition, respectively (Fig. 1; Eq. 1, 3b).
595 
596 Maximum plant mass is the second trait that describes
597 plant geometry and is positively related to actual plant
598 mass, according to the sigmoidal growth equation (Eq. 4).
599 In the two-layer version equal maximal masses for shoot
600 and root are assumed. Individual susceptibility to grazing
601 is modelled as a function of fleaf and mshoot (Eq. 7) as
602 higher and larger plants tend to be more affected by
603 grazing (Diaz et al. 2001).
604 
605 Maximum plant mass and individual seed mass are
606 assumed to be positively correlated and combined within a
607 trait syndrome (Eriksson and Jakobsson 1998). The welldocumented
608 seed size versus seed number tradeoff emerges
609 in our model, because PFTs with higher seed mass produce
610 less seeds from the same amount of reproductive mass
611 (Leishman 2001, Schippers et al. 2001, Westoby et al.
612 2002). The disadvantage of a low seed number is balanced
613 by the higher recruitment success of large seedlings, due to
614 the weighted lottery of seedling competition and the higher
615 initial mass of plants that germinated from large seeds
616 (Jakobsson and Eriksson 2000). A negative correlation
617 between seed mass and dispersal distance was assumed
618 here, which is supported at least for wind-dispersed seeds
619 (Jongejans and Schippers 1999). For simplicity, equal
620 values are used for mean and standard deviation of the
621 dispersal kernel assuming a higher variance for the dispersal
622 distances of smaller seeds.
623 
624 The response of plants to different resource conditions is
625 distinguished into categories reflecting the tradeoff between
626 competitive ability and stress tolerance (Grime 2001).
627 
628 Maximum growth rate in resource-rich environments is
629 given as the maximum resource utilization per shoot/root
630 area (rumax), while stress resistance is specified by the
631 maximal number of weeks a plant can survive under
632 resource stress exposure (survmax). Accordingly, PFTs with
633 high rumax feature a low stress resistance and vice versa.
634 
635 We distinguished two strategies of response to grazing:
636 (1) grazing tolerance by fast re-growth of removed biomass
637 and (2) grazing avoidance by low palatability through
638 defence structures or secondary compounds (Bullock et al.
639 2001). In our model, the relationship between leaf mass and
640 leaf area is given by the parameter cshoot (Eq. 1). This
641 parameter is functionally analogous to the specific leaf area
642 (SLA), which was proposed by Westoby et al. (2002) as a
643 key trait to characterize plant strategies. High SLA is related
644 to high efficiency of light interception and fast growth,
645 while leaves with low SLA show higher longevity, structural
646 strength or high allocation to defensive compounds
647 (Westoby et al. 2002). Accordingly, a high cshoot value for
648 grazing tolerance versus a low value for grazing avoidance is
649 used here. The positive effect of defence compounds is
650 expressed as a low palatability (palat) and thus, a low
651 susceptibility towards grazing (Eq. 7).
652 
653 For each of the four functional traits three attributes
654 were distinguished. The parameter values used should
655 represent the range of contrasting plant strategies with
656 respect to resource response, competitive ability and
657 grazing response that could potentially occur in grassland
658 ecosystems. Combining all three attributes of all four
659 functional traits allowed us to define 34 by 81 PFTs in total
660 (Table 3).
